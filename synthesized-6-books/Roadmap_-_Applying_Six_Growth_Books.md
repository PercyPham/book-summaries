# The Build Roadmap: Putting Six Growth Books Into Practice

A sequential path from zero to a running customer-acquisition machine, synthesized from *Sell Like Crazy*, *$100M Leads*, *DotCom Secrets*, *Traffic Secrets*, *Oversubscribed*, and *The SaaS Playbook*.

The order is not arbitrary. Each step produces the prerequisite the next one needs: you can't design bait until you have a core offer to point it at, can't price traffic until you know your economics, can't scale traffic until the funnel holds water, and can't delegate until the machine works with you running it. Do them out of order and you'll pour money into a leaky funnel or advertise an offer nobody wants.

**How to read each step:** every step lists what to have in hand before starting (*Prerequisites*), the calls only you can make (*Decisions*), what usually goes wrong here (*Risks*), and how to tell it's working or not (*Signals*).

---

## PHASE A — Foundation (before you build a single asset)

### Step 1 — Adopt the economic frame and pick your game

*Why first:* every downstream choice is justified by one law, and you can't make trade-offs coherently until you've internalized it.

The law: **the business that can afford to spend the most to acquire a customer wins.** You earn that ability by raising lifetime value and recovering acquisition cost fast — not by finding cheaper clicks. Before building anything, decide the shape of the business this machine serves.

- **Decisions:** Volume model or premium/scarcity model? (Hormozi-style lead volume vs. Priestley-style capacity-constrained demand — this colors pricing, traffic, and launch style throughout.) Lifestyle scale or high-performance scale? Bootstrapped/self-funded or funded — and if funded, is it a *tool to buy time*, not an identity?
- **Risks:** Skipping straight to tactics (ads, funnels) without the economic frame — you'll optimize headlines on a structurally unprofitable offer. Trying to jump from "struggle" directly to "high-performance empire" without first proving a profitable small version.
- **Signals right:** You can finish the sentence "I win because I can spend more than competitors to acquire a customer, because ______." **Signal wrong:** your growth plan is "get cheaper traffic."

### Step 2 — Define the dream customer and the ultimate result

*Why here:* specificity created now propagates into every asset — the words in your ads, the bait, the offer, where you advertise. Vague input here corrupts everything downstream.

- **Prerequisites:** A way to talk to or observe real prospects (calls, communities, existing customers).
- **Decisions:** *Who* is the dream buyer (a detailed avatar, not demographics)? *Which single core desire* do you serve — **Health, Wealth, or Relationships** — and is the customer moving *away from pain* or *toward pleasure*? *Where* do they already gather online? What is the *ultimate result* you deliver (the top of the ladder)? Are you willing to be **"famous to a few"** thousand rather than bland to everyone?
- **Risks:** Targeting "everyone" to maximize reach — it produces generic messaging that converts no one. Stacking multiple core desires into one message (cuts conversions by ~90%). Describing what you *sell* instead of the *result* you deliver.
- **Signals right:** You can write down dozens of exact phrases your prospect says to themselves about their problem, and you know the top ~4% of customers who'd drive the majority of revenue. **Signal wrong:** you can't name where your customer congregates or what they'd type into Google at 2am.

## PHASE B — Architecture (build the assets, in dependency order)

### Step 3 — Build the value ladder and prove the unit economics

*Why before bait and traffic:* bait exists to feed a ladder, and traffic budget is set by the math. Both are undefined until this exists.

- **Prerequisites:** Step 2's "ultimate result" (defines the top rung).
- **Decisions:** Map rungs from bait → frontend → middle → backend → **continuity (recurring revenue)**. Identify the "missing rungs" you must invent. Set price points, and for each, note the *funnel type and selling environment* it will need (cheap = self-serve page; mid = webinar-length argument; high-ticket = application + phone). For SaaS/subscription: pick a **value metric** (seats, usage, subscribers) so customers pay more as they succeed (*expansion revenue*).
- **Risks:** No continuity/recurring rung — you rebuild revenue from zero every month. Pricing below your sales cost: a $10–15/mo product with a human sales process is unviable; if *nobody complains* about your price, it's too low.
- **Signals right:** You can state target LTV (or annual contract value), and a credible path where front-end purchases (bumps + upsells) recover ad cost within the first transaction or first 30 days. There's a natural reason a happy customer wants to pay you *more* for *more* result. **Signal wrong:** you can't say how much you can afford to spend to acquire a customer.

### Step 4 — Engineer the irresistible offer and reverse the risk

*Why here:* a strong offer melts objections a great funnel can't; a weak offer fails even with perfect traffic. The core offer must be sharp before you build the bait that leads to it.

- **Prerequisites:** The frontend/core rung from Step 3.
- **Decisions:** Craft a **Godfather Offer** — a bold, specific promise (e.g., "result X in Y weeks or you get $Z"). Translate every *feature* into a *benefit*. Add a **risk-reversing guarantee** whose job is to make the buyer feel safe. Add the "second yes" mechanics: an order-form **bump** and a **One-Time Offer** (the next logical thing, never more of the same). Consider a **Free-Plus-Shipping** or trial/"invisible" structure to shatter the entry barrier.
- **Risks:** Listing features instead of benefits. A vague guarantee. An OTO that offers "more of what they just bought." Discounting instead of increasing perceived value (use "the Stack" — total the value repeatedly so the price feels small).
- **Signals right:** A cold reader would call it a no-brainer; the guarantee makes *you* slightly nervous. **Signal wrong:** the offer needs heavy persuasion to seem worth it.

### Step 5 — Create the bait (lead magnet / HVCO)

*Why after the offer:* the bait must solve a narrow problem whose solution *reveals the exact problem your core offer fixes*. You can't engineer that link until the core offer exists.

- **Prerequisites:** A functioning core offer (Step 4).
- **Decisions:** Pick one narrow problem in the "problem–solution cycle." Choose the type: reveal a hidden problem (audit/diagnosis), give a sample of the core offer, or give step one of a multi-step process. Choose format for frictionless consumption (audio/text/video). Write an **irresistible title** — the single biggest driver of whether anyone takes it. End with a **call to action** that says exactly what to do next, with a reason to act *now* (scarcity/urgency).
- **Risks:** Giving away "sucky fluff" — the market judges your paid value by your free value. Making it hard to consume. No CTA to the next step. (SaaS caveat: a one-time lead magnet is an asset; a permanent free *product tier* is a recurring cost that attracts tire-kickers — don't confuse the two.)
- **Signals right:** People actually consume it and *ask* for the next step unprompted. **Signal wrong:** downloads with no engagement and no forward motion.

### Step 6 — Build capture and start owning the audience

*Why here:* bait is worthless until it converts rented attention into an audience you control. This is the pivot from *traffic you control/don't control* to *traffic you own*.

- **Prerequisites:** The bait (Step 5).
- **Decisions:** Build a dedicated **opt-in / squeeze page** whose only job is to trade the bait for name + email. Add ultra-curious *fascination bullets*. Ask for the minimum fields possible.
- **Risks:** Sending traffic to a homepage or straight to a sales page (like proposing marriage on a first date). Asking for too much info (kills conversion). Building your list on rented ground you don't own.
- **Signals right:** A healthy opt-in rate and a growing owned list. **Signal wrong:** high page traffic, few opt-ins → the bait or the bullets aren't compelling enough.

### Step 7 — Build the nurture / follow-up engine

*Why here:* most of the market (the ~97% not ready today) needs trust built before any ask. This engine runs automatically against the list you're now capturing.

- **Prerequisites:** An owned list (Step 6) and the offer to point toward (Step 4).
- **Decisions:** Define an **Attractive Character** (backstory, flaws, a polarizing stance — people bond with people, not logos). Load an onboarding sequence that *bonds* (dramatic, open-loop "soap opera" style), then ongoing near-daily emails that are mostly *entertainment/story* with a soft tie to an offer. For the conversion push, sequence the psychology: **Emotion → Logic → Fear** (story, then justification/risk-reversal, then urgency). Optionally deliver small wins for free first (help them cross the first hurdles) so skepticism is gone before the call.
- **Risks:** 100% educational content emails → boredom, unopened, dead list. Corporate/impersonal voice. Heavy-HTML "commercial-looking" emails that hit spam/promotions — prefer plain, personal-looking mail and short lowercase subject lines.
- **Signals right:** Opens and replies stay high; people feel they *know* you; the list generates roughly **$1 per subscriber per month**. **Signal wrong:** open rates decay week over week.

## PHASE C — Traffic (fill the machine — only now)

### Step 8 — Map the congregations, pick ONE channel, and model what works

*Why after the funnel:* traffic sent into a funnel that can't capture or convert just burns money faster. Now that capture + nurture exist, you fill them.

- **Prerequisites:** A working capture + nurture path (Steps 6–7).
- **Decisions:** Build a **Dream 100** — the influencers, groups, podcasts, and lists that already gathered your audience (look one level out, into the broader *submarket*). Pick **one** primary platform — ideally the one you already use and whose native language you understand — and commit. Place your channels on Hormozi's **Core Four** (warm outreach, free content, cold outreach, paid ads) and plan to start warm/free and move toward cold/paid. **Model, don't invent:** reverse-engineer working competitors across five variables (demographics, offer, landing page, traffic source, ad copy) and use "Judo" against incumbents' weaknesses.
- **Risks:** Attempting omnipresence across every platform too early — stay on one until you cross ~$1M. Inventing funnels from scratch instead of modeling proven ones.
- **Signals right:** You've catalogued a reachable ecosystem in the hundreds of thousands to a million+ prospects, on one platform. **Signal wrong:** effort spread thin across five channels, none mastered.

### Step 9 — Turn on traffic: earned + controlled, into a break-even front end

*Why here:* with the funnel proven, you scale reach — earned for trust, paid for speed — and design it to pay for itself.

- **Prerequisites:** Chosen channel + Dream 100 (Step 8); front-end offer with upsells (Steps 3–4).
- **Decisions:** *Work your way in* (earned): serve the Dream 100 before asking — "dig your well before you're thirsty" — via guest spots, collabs, answering questions without pitching. *Buy your way in* (controlled): run **prospecting ads** (cold, many creative "hooks," expect ~80% of budget for ~20% of early results) whose real job is to fill **retargeting** buckets — *Engaged → Landed → Owned* — served escalating Emotion/Logic/Fear ads. Every ad's job is to **sell the click**, not the product. Route everything into the break-even funnel so upsells recover ad spend (**Client-Financed Acquisition**).
- **Risks:** Depending on a single traffic source (one algorithm change ends you). Sending cold, problem-unaware traffic straight to a sales page — build a **pre-frame bridge** (article/advertorial, jargon translated to their language) first. Spending before tracking is in place.
- **Signals right:** **LTGP:CAC > 3:1**, and profit in the first 30 days covers acquisition cost (ad budget effectively becomes infinite). **Signal wrong:** you're going cash-negative because upsells aren't liquidating ad spend.

### Step 10 — Convert: sell like a doctor (for anything high-ticket)

*Why here:* low-ticket closes in the funnel; mid/high-ticket needs a changed selling environment (phone/application) and a consultative close.

- **Prerequisites:** Booked calls/applications from the nurture + traffic engine.
- **Decisions:** **Diagnose before prescribing** — ask for their "why," their desired 12-month outcome, and their specific pain before pitching. Deliver a small piece of value live (an "appetizer"). Pre-qualify hard (filter by budget/fit) so you don't burn time on bad-fit prospects. Then prescribe the Godfather Offer and state price with conviction.
- **Risks:** Pitching before diagnosing ("a prescription without a diagnosis is malpractice"). Feature-tour demos. High-pressure tactics on a warm, pre-sold lead. Running demos for unqualified prospects.
- **Signals right:** Prospects talk themselves into needing the solution; close rates hold without pressure. **Signal wrong:** you're "convincing" reluctant, unqualified people.

## PHASE D — Amplify and scale (make it bigger, then make it run without you)

### Step 11 — Optimize: volume first, then conversion, then new

*Why here:* before adding channels or blaming the market, wring out what you have — in the right order.

- **Decisions:** Apply **More → Better → New.** *More:* most failures are volume failures — radically increase inputs first (Rule of 100 / "Open to Goal": work to a number of *outcomes*, not hours). *Better:* find the single funnel step with the worst drop-off (the constraint) and A/B test **one variable** there. Fix the funnel **bottom-up** — retention/churn first, then trial-to-paid/conversion, then top-of-funnel traffic. Rank experiments with **ICE** (Impact, Confidence, Ease) and log every test's CAC. *New:* only after More and Better plateau, add a placement/platform/channel.
- **Risks:** The **"Size of the Pie" fallacy** — declaring a market saturated after one ad type on one platform. Changing multiple variables at once (you learn nothing). Pouring traffic into a leaky funnel.
- **Signals right:** Each constraint you fix lifts the whole funnel's throughput. **Signal wrong:** you keep jumping to new platforms while the current one is under-exploited.

### Step 12 — Engineer demand and remarkability

*Why here:* with a working funnel, you can now command a premium and lower acquisition cost toward zero by making customers do your marketing.

- **Decisions:** Set a strict **capacity** (the number you can serve to genuine delight) and refuse to expand it on demand. Create **transparency of movement** — waitlists, visible interest, celebrating *clients* (not yourself) so prospects see the herd moving. **Polarize** (stand clearly for/against something). Consider a **campaign** rhythm (build demand → release only when interest far exceeds capacity, roughly 5× for deposit-level signals up to 100× for free opt-ins → over-deliver → debrief → rest). Make delivery **"positively remarkable"** (promise ~70%, surprise with the rest) so word-of-mouth replaces ad spend.
- **Risks:** Greedily expanding capacity or raising price mid-rush (destroys the scarcity you built). Being bland to please everyone. Treating "satisfied" as enough — satisfied customers don't talk; *delighted* ones do.
- **Signals right:** Demand visibly outstrips supply; rejected buyers become advocates; referrals climb. **Signal wrong:** you're chasing prospects instead of them pursuing you.

### Step 13 — Scale through leverage: lead getters, team, and moats

*Why here:* the machine works with you running it — now decouple growth from your hours. Pursue the four allies in their natural order.

- **Decisions:** **Customers** first — engineer referrals with two-sided incentives (only exponential channel; requires a product that creates real goodwill). Then **Employees** — hire complete *roles* (not scattered tasks), starting with what you're bad at or dislike; train via *Document–Demonstrate–Duplicate*; hire *project-* and *owner-level* thinkers. Then **Agencies** — rent briefly to *buy a new skill and transfer it in-house*, not to depend on forever. Then **Affiliates** — an army with rich commissions (up to ~50–100% of front-end), done-for-you assets, and **integration marketing** (your offer on their thank-you/exit pages). Build **moats** so retention holds: integrations, brand/positioning, switching costs, owned traffic — because features get copied ("false moat"). Align team incentives with **profit-sharing over equity** (reserve equity for a co-founder).
- **Risks:** Scaling referrals/affiliates on a mediocre product (nothing to refer). Confusing an *advertising* problem (not enough qualified leads) with a *sales* problem (leads don't close). Permanent agency dependence. "Siren songs" (translating the app, white-labeling, new verticals) that look like growth but add burden.
- **Signals right:** Monthly referral rate exceeds churn (automatic compounding); for subscriptions, **net-negative churn** (expansion revenue > lost revenue) — MRR grows even with zero new customers. **Signal wrong:** every new customer still requires your personal effort.

### Step 14 — Protect the operator and play the long game

*Why last and ongoing:* the binding constraint is you. The machine only compounds if the person running it doesn't burn out or quit early.

- **Decisions:** Spend your time on **Risks** (unknowns: product-market fit, new channels) and delegate **Certainties** (routine, known-path work). Reframe "roadblocks" as "speed bumps" with a Plan B/C/D. Use funding and mastermind peers as *tools* to buy time and borrow judgment. Take periodic unplugged **founder retreats** to re-align work with what brings joy. Reinvest a large share of profit back into traffic. Ask daily: "how do I give myself a raise today?"
- **Risks:** Burnout (living at "needed + good-at + zero-joy"). Strategy-hopping — abandoning a method after early losses; a real machine takes years. Expecting overnight success.
- **Signals right:** You keep "rolling the die" at volume with patience — a winning outcome becomes a statistical inevitability. **Signal wrong:** you're changing the whole strategy every few months.

## The dependency chain at a glance

Economic frame → **dream customer** → **value ladder + economics** → **irresistible offer** → **bait** → **capture/own the list** → **nurture** → **congregations + one channel** → **turn on traffic (break-even)** → **consultative close** → **optimize (more→better→new)** → **engineer demand + remarkability** → **leverage (customers→employees→agencies→affiliates + moats)** → **protect the operator**.

Each arrow is a prerequisite, not a suggestion. The through-logic: you define *who* and *what result* so you can build a *ladder* worth ascending; you make the *offer* irresistible so *bait* can lead to it; you *capture and nurture* because most buyers aren't ready today; you *fill* the machine with traffic only once it holds water and pays for itself; you *amplify* by fixing constraints and engineering demand; and you *scale* by handing the working machine to other people and systems — all sustained by an operator who plays long enough for the math to become inevitable.
